# Evaluation criteria for term projects

## Why do we have these criteria?

Before discussing the evaluation criteria, I will briefly mention how you can learn and practice data analytics.

First, you should play with data, especially with real data. Data science is NOT something that you can learn just by understanding the underlying theories and applying them to some examples. There are so many pitfalls and uncertainties in data analysis, and you should learn how to avoid them and find good answers for better decision making by doing.

Second, you should see how other people's analysis. If you just google it, you can find many good analysis, often in jupyter notebook format. You can learn not just data analysis techniques, but more importantly thinking processes from others' analysis. (Well, people seldom share their mistakes during EDA or other steps of data analysis.)

Last but not the least, you should keep asking questions in the above steps. Asking good questions is often the first step of good data analysis. And you may need to ask numerous questions to have good ones, especially when you are not familiar with data science or the target domain.

## Who evaluates and how different evaluations are summed up?

Having said that, the following is the evaluation criteria for your term projects. Each student should evaluate the analysis of all the teams (except your own team) with the following criteria in 1-5 scale. TA and instructor will evaluate with the same criteria. Weighted sum of all these evaluation and the evaluation of final reports (only evaluated by TA and instructor) will be the final evaluation of your projects.

Finally, you can give any comments or suggestions. For good ones, additional points may be given.

## Evaluation criteria

1. Topic
  - How do you like the topic?
  - Is it practical, interesting and unique?

2. Data
  - Do they used legitimate?
  - Is the size of datasets big enough for generalization?
  - Do they use all the relevant data?

3. Analysis
  - Is the analysis process rigid?
  - Are their hypothesis tests valid?
  - Or did they apply machine learning techniques properly?
  - Are their analysis free from bias?

4. Practicality
  - Did they find a new or important insights and/or knowledge?
  - Can their analysis be a basis for clear decision making?
  - Did they properly mention limitation of their analysis, and if possible, the remedies of such limitations?

5. Presentation
  - Is the presentation (both presentation materials and speech) clear to understand?
  - Do they handle Q&A properly? Did they understand the issue and give the relevant answers?
